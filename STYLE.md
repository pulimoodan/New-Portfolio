# Writing style — Akbar Ali (@pulimoodan)

A description of how I actually write, derived from my Medium essays and dev.to posts.
Useful as a personal reference, or paste it into an AI as a style brief when drafting.

---

## The core of it

I write like I'm thinking out loud to a friend, and then apologizing for it.
Confident sentence, immediate self-puncture. My Medium bio is the whole style in five words:
**"Writes a lot, but bluffs a lot."**

---

## Rules

### 1. The parenthetical aside is the signature move

Make a claim, then interrupt yourself in parentheses to undercut, joke, hedge, or confess.
A second, quieter voice arguing with the first one. Use it often — at least once every few paragraphs.

> Hit the gym (I procastinate that too)
> Sleep well (Ah, I can't)
> Avoid excessive sugar and caffeine (chatgpt said it would work)
> Translate any language, with any iso codes: like pt or pt-BR (because, we're using AI. Not a flex btw)
> I don't feel like starting a conversation with someone who looks like smarter than me (ofcourse, they're)

Never let a confident statement stand completely alone.

### 2. Open on a scene, not a thesis

Start with a body in a room. Something physical, ordinary, slightly boring. Extract the idea *after*.

- A bowl of rice balls in Mom's kitchen → an essay about why we automate things.
- Walking down the street with a colleague looking for food → an essay about evaluating employers.
- Standing in a tech event I didn't earn an invitation to → an essay about imposter syndrome.

No throat-clearing. No "In today's fast-paced world."

### 3. Write vertically

One or two sentences, then white space. Paragraphs are beats, not containers.
When it matters, break mid-thought onto its own line and let it hang.

> The list of `PR`s to be reviewed, getting long and long each day in numbers.
>
> The dashboard, looking at me with that weird devil laugh every time I open the Github.
>
> It's not me, being lazy and stubborn to keep reviewing everything consistently (maybe).

### 4. Be self-deprecating, specifically

Not vague modesty — concrete, funny insults aimed at myself.

> my damn small brain's cheap ideas
> learn more about why I am still a loser
> I should yap more about my success (still there aren't any)
> making games as a hobby (I must say, I'm not an expert)

### 5. Drop one aphorism per piece

Among the casual lines, land one polished one. This is the poet showing up. Don't force more than one or two.

> End of the timeline, we're not saving time — we're finding the unhealthiest ways to waste time.
> Someone just gambles, others commit with conviction.
> People being developers instead of poets (The art of beautiful code is long gone).

### 6. End by opening a door, never closing one

No summary, no conclusion. Ask the reader something. Invite them in. Credit people freely.

> Have you ever got into a situation like this in your life?
> Anyone interested to contribute?
> Comment your opinions and doubts.
> thanks to @efpage for the feedback

### 7. Speak, don't write

Spoken register. Swear the way people swear in conversation — for emphasis, not shock.
"Nah", "yap", "slop", "OMG", "a piece of shit", "the bloody damn internet breaking algorithm",
"Work your magic, cook well", "Slam a PR".

### 8. Moralize, but hide it under jokes

I have opinions about craft, laziness, AI-driven FOMO, and doing things with your own hands.
Make the argument — but never preach. Wrap it in self-mockery so it stays likeable.

---

## Two modes

**Technical posts** — ruthlessly compressed. `Problem → Figuring out → Solution`. Sometimes 40 words
and two code blocks, and that's the whole post. Write the post I wish Google had handed me.
Open cold: *"Spent a lot of time debugging this."*
The personality still leaks into the bullets — keep the jokes.

**Personal essays** — loose, conversational, searching. Longer. Don't resolve; trail off into a question.

---

## Titles

Short, plain, often a question. No colons, no listicles, no hype.

> How big is it? · Code pollution · This or that? · An alternative for Render? · Why should it be automated

---

## Mechanics to fix (not part of the voice)

The rhythm, the interruptions and the register are mine. These are just errors — clean them up:

- Spelling: `ofcourse` → of course, `eventhough` → even though, `noone` → no one,
  `procastinate` → procrastinate, `sorrounded` → surrounded, `loadash` → lodash
- Pluralized mass nouns: "your feedbacks" → your feedback, "most of the medias" → most of the media
- Subject–verb agreement: "My colleague and I *was* walking" → were walking; "What *are* your vision" → What is your vision
- Double past tense: "Didn't *made* any projects" → didn't make
- "insisting me to do it" → pushing me to do it; "suggest me more videos" → suggest more videos to me

---

## Forgery test

Open on an ordinary physical scene. Break paragraphs after every sentence or two. State something,
then puncture it in parentheses. Insult yourself once. Drop one line that sounds like it belongs on a poster.
Refuse to conclude — ask the reader a question instead.
